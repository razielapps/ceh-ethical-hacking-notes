Topic 60: Social Engineering with BlackEye

BlackEye is a powerful, open-source tool written in Python that automates the process of creating and managing phishing campaigns. It is designed to be a "one-stop-shop" for simulating credential harvesting attacks, making it a valuable tool for ethical hackers to demonstrate the ease and effectiveness of phishing to a client.

At its core, BlackEye does one thing very well: it clones the login pages of popular websites and sets up a server to host these fake pages. When a victim enters their credentials into the fake page, BlackEye captures them and displays them in real-time to the attacker.

**How BlackEye Works (The Attack Flow):**

1.  **Setup:** The attacker runs BlackEye on their machine (e.g., Kali Linux). The tool presents a menu with dozens of templates for popular sites like Google, Facebook, Instagram, Netflix, Microsoft, and many more.
2.  **Cloning:** The attacker selects a template (e.g., `10` for Google). BlackEye sets up a local web server hosting a near-perfect copy of the Google login page.
3.  **Delivery:** The attacker now needs to get the victim to visit their fake page. Since it's running on the attacker's local machine, they use a tunneling service like **Ngrok** or **LocalTunnel**. These services create a public, temporary URL (e.g., `https://abc123.ngrok.io`) that redirects traffic to the attacker's local server. This URL is what the attacker sends to the victim via a phishing email or message.
4.  **The Trap:** The victim clicks the link, sees what looks like the real Google login page, and enters their username and password.
5.  **Capture and Reporting:** The credentials are posted to the attacker's server. BlackEye instantly logs the credentials to its terminal and can also save them to a file. Some versions may even send a notification to the attacker's Discord or Telegram.

**Key Components and Concepts:**

- **Ngrok:** A critical tool used alongside BlackEye. It exposes a local web server to the internet with a public HTTPS URL, bypassing firewalls and NAT.
- **Templates:** The collection of cloned login pages. The realism of these templates is what makes the attack convincing.
- **Social Engineering:** The tool itself is just technology. The success of the attack depends entirely on the phishing message (the "lure") that convinces the victim to click the link. This could be a fake security alert, a promise of a free gift, or a notification about a shared document.

**The Ethical Hacker's Use Case:**

A professional penetration tester would use BlackEye in a controlled, authorized assessment.
- They would craft a targeted phishing email (a "spear-phishing" email) tailored to the client's employees.
- They would use BlackEye to host a clone of the client's own corporate login portal (like Office 365).
- The results provide the client with a clear, measurable metric: "X% of employees clicked the link, and Y% entered their credentials." This is powerful data to justify investments in security awareness training.

**A Critical Legal and Ethical Warning:**
Using a tool like BlackEye to phish anyone without their explicit, prior consent is illegal. It is a form of fraud and identity theft. It should only be used on systems and people you own, or as part of a formal, written penetration testing agreement.

Glossary for Topic 60:

BlackEye: An open-source tool used to automate phishing attacks by cloning login pages and capturing submitted credentials.

Phishing: A cyberattack that uses disguised sites or emails to trick individuals into revealing sensitive information.

Ngrok: A tool that creates a secure tunnel from a public endpoint to a locally running web service, allowing it to be accessed over the internet.

Template: In the context of BlackEye, a pre-made clone of a popular website's login page.

Credential Harvesting: The process of collecting usernames and passwords, typically through a phishing attack.

Spear Phishing: A highly targeted phishing attack directed at specific individuals or companies.

Tunneling: The process of encapsulating a network protocol within another protocol, in this case, used to expose a local server to the internet.

3.  **The Delivery Mechanism:**
    - BlackEye runs a server on your local machine (e.g., on `127.0.0.1`). Why can't you just send the IP `127.0.0.1` to the victim?
    - Answer: "Because `127.0.0.1` is the localhost address, meaning 'this computer.' It will only work on the machine running the server. The victim's computer cannot reach your localhost address over the internet."

4.  **The Ngrok Solution:**
    - What is the specific purpose of using Ngrok in this attack?
    - Answer: "Ngrok creates a public, internet-accessible URL (like `abc123.ngrok.io`) that tunnels all traffic back to the local BlackEye server running on my machine, allowing the victim to access my fake page from anywhere."

5.  **Post-Attack Analysis:**
